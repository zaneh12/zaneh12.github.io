Visible Market Orders Predict Trader Beliefs¶

We estimate three belief distributions from the order book:

  • Latent: what traders actually believe.
  • Filtered: what shows up as standing orders.
  • Rejection: how traders strategically place orders under spread and risk constraints.

Granger tests show:

  • Filtered and rejection beliefs predict future latent beliefs — meaning the visible part of the book contains real information about what traders privately believe.
  • Rejection behavior also predicts future filtered orders — showing that strategic placement shapes what becomes visible.

In short, even though standing orders are filtered and strategic, they still help forecast what traders really think — and should be modeled together.

Example¶

If a surge of high-priced standing buy orders appears (filtered belief shifts upward),
and those orders are placed with unusually sharp rejection patterns (tight spread, high skew),
then our model predicts that underlying trader beliefs (latent) will drift upward in future snapshots —
even if the current price hasn’t moved yet.

Estimation Procedure¶

We estimate the latent belief distribution behind a prediction market order book using a three-stage Beta fitting pipeline.

Step 1: Histogram-Based Fit (Filtered Beta)¶

  • Group visible limit orders by price, summing size at each level.
  • Normalize sizes to construct a discrete density.
  • Approximate a sample by repeating prices proportional to density.
  • Fit a Beta distribution on $[0,1]$ using scipy.stats.beta.fit with floc=0, fscale=1.

This yields:

$$ \text{Filtered: } \hat{\alpha}, \hat{\beta} $$

Step 2: Rejection Sharpening (Rejection Beta)¶

  • Compute spread: $\text{spread} = \text{ask}_{\min} - \text{bid}_{\max}$
  • Compute imbalance: $z = \frac{\text{bid size}}{\text{bid + ask size}}$, normalized as $z' = (z - 0.5) \cdot 2$
  • Define sharpening multiplier:
$$ \text{sharp} = \min(sharpness~factor,\ \max(1.5,\ 0.25 / \text{spread})) $$
  • Apply sharpening and skew:
$$ \alpha_r = \hat{\alpha} \cdot \text{sharp} \cdot (1 + 0.1 z') \\ \beta_r = \hat{\beta} \cdot \text{sharp} \cdot (1 - 0.1 z') $$

This defines the rejection filter distribution used to simulate order visibility.

Step 3: Inverse Simulation (Latent Belief Recovery)¶

  • Simulate beliefs $x_i \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$.
  • Accept each $x_i$ with probability:
$$ 1 - \frac{f_r(x_i)}{\max f_r} $$

where $f_r$ is the rejection PDF using $(\alpha_r, \beta_r)$.

  • Fit a Beta distribution to the accepted values to get $(\alpha^*, \beta^*)$.
  • Minimize:
$$ \left( \alpha^* - \hat{\alpha} \right)^2 + \left( \beta^* - \hat{\beta} \right)^2 $$

to recover the latent $(\alpha, \beta)$ that would have been filtered into the observed distribution.

Outputs per Snapshot¶

  • Filtered belief: $(\hat{\alpha}, \hat{\beta})$
  • Rejection filter: $(\alpha_r, \beta_r)$
  • Latent belief: $(\alpha, \beta)$

With means:

$$ \mu = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} $$

We track all three means along with spread, imbalance, midpoint, and VWAP for diagnostic analysis.
