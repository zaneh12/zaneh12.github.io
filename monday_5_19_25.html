2. Belief Reconstruction¶

  • Let:
    • $$ \text{observed}(p) \propto \text{belief}(p) \cdot \text{trading}(p) $$
    • $$ \text{belief}(p) \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta) $$
    • $$ \text{trading}(p) \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha', \beta') $$
  • Goal: infer $(\alpha, \beta)$ given observed distribution and a trading filter
  • Model $(\alpha', \beta')$ as functions of microstructure:
    $$ \alpha', \beta' = f(\text{spread}, \text{imbalance}, \text{midpoint}) $$

Let $Y_t$ be a $k$-dimensional vector time series following a VAR(1) model:

$$ Y_t = A Y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t, \quad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma) $$

The impulse response function (IRF) at horizon $h$ is defined as:

$$ \text{IRF}(h) = \frac{\partial Y_{t+h}}{\partial \varepsilon_t} $$

It measures the effect of a one-unit shock to the innovation $\varepsilon_t$ on the future value $Y_{t+h}$, holding all else constant.

Interpreting Impulse Response Function (IRF) Values¶

  • 0 (Flat Line at Zero)
    The shock has no effect on that variable — the system is unresponsive. This is the baseline or "nothing happens" case.

  • +1 or -1 at Lag 0
    The shock itself is usually a 1-unit shock, so you may see an immediate jump of +1 in the shocked variable. Other variables may respond with smaller positive or negative values.

  • Positive IRF (> 0)
    A positive response means the variable increases in response to the shock.
    Example: If IRF_{filtered, observed} is 0.2, then a 1-unit shock to observed leads to a +0.2 change in filtered.

  • Negative IRF (< 0)
    A negative response means the variable decreases in response to the shock.
    This could indicate mean-reversion, price correction, or opposing force.

  • Persistent Response
    If the IRF stays significantly above or below zero for several time steps, it means the shock has a lasting effect.
    This often implies structural influence — the variables are meaningfully linked.

  • Transient / Fading Response
    If the IRF quickly returns to zero, the shock has a short-term effect only.
    This is typical in systems with little memory or quick adjustment.

  • Oscillating Response (up-down-up...)
    This suggests the system overreacts then corrects, possibly due to feedback loops or damping mechanisms.

  • Wide Confidence Bands
    Means the estimates are noisy or uncertain — be cautious interpreting the direction or strength of the effect.
