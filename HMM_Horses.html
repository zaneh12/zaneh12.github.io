Hidden Markov Model (HMM)

The Hidden Markov Model is a probabilistic model that deals with sequences of observable events and their underlying hidden states.

Mathematically, it can be represented as follows:

Given a sequence of observations \( O = o_1, o_2, ..., o_T \), and a sequence of hidden states \( S = s_1, s_2, ..., s_T \), the joint probability of the observed sequence and the hidden states is given by:

\[ P(O, S | \lambda) = P(s_1 | \pi) \times \prod_{t=2}^{T} P(s_t | s_{t-1}, A) \times \prod_{t=1}^{T} P(o_t | s_t, B) \]

Where:

  • \( \lambda \) represents the parameters of the model (initial state probabilities \( \pi \), state transition probabilities \( A \), and observation probabilities \( B \)).
  • \( P(s_t | s_{t-1}, A) \) is the probability of transitioning from state \( s_{t-1} \) to state \( s_t \).
  • \( P(o_t | s_t, B) \) is the probability of observing \( o_t \) given the current state \( s_t \).

K Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Model

The K Nearest Neighbors model is a non-parametric, lazy learning algorithm used for classification and regression. It classifies a data point based on the majority class among its \( k \) nearest neighbors in the feature space.

Mathematically, the prediction of a new data point \( x \) can be represented as follows:

Given a set of training data \( D = \{(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), ..., (x_N, y_N)\} \), where \( x_i \) represents the feature vector and \( y_i \) represents the class label, the predicted class label for a new data point \( x \) is determined by a majority vote among its \( k \) nearest neighbors in the feature space.

Ensembling HMM and KNN

To ensemble the Hidden Markov Model and the K Nearest Neighbors model, one possible approach is to use the output probabilities or predictions from each model as features for a higher-level classifier, such as logistic regression or a neural network. This higher-level classifier then combines the outputs of the individual models to make a final prediction.

Mathematically, the ensemble prediction can be represented as follows:

Given the output probabilities \( P_{HMM}(S|O) \) from the HMM and the predictions \( \hat{y}_{KNN} \) from the KNN model, a higher-level classifier can be trained to learn a weighted combination of these outputs to make the final prediction.

Back-to-Lay Horse Betting Strategy

Parameter Value

Here's how the strategy works:

  1. Initially, we back the horse "Speedy" with $100 at odds of 10.00, risking $100 for a potential profit of $900 if the horse wins.
  2. Then, if the odds on the exchange shorten (decrease), say to 4.00, we can place a lay bet at those lower odds.
  3. Let's calculate our potential profit/loss at different scenarios:

Scenario 1: Horse Wins

Total Profit $600

Scenario 2: Horse Loses
