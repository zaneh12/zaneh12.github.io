How the AMM Makes Money: LMSR Explained¶

Polymarket's AMM uses a Logarithmic Market Scoring Rule (LMSR) to price shares dynamically based on trader demand. The AMM adjusts prices according to a convex cost function that guarantees liquidity and caps worst-case loss.

Let $q_i$ be the number of outstanding shares on outcome $i$. The LMSR cost function is:

$$ C(\mathbf{q}) = b \cdot \log\left( \sum_{i=1}^n \exp\left( \frac{q_i}{b} \right) \right) $$

where:

  • $\mathbf{q} = (q_1, \dots, q_n)$ is the vector of shares,
  • $b > 0$ is the liquidity parameter (higher $b$ = deeper book).

The price for outcome $i$ is the gradient of the cost function:

$$ p_i = \frac{\partial C}{\partial q_i} = \frac{\exp\left( \frac{q_i}{b} \right)}{\sum_{j=1}^n \exp\left( \frac{q_j}{b} \right)} $$

Profit Mechanism¶

The AMM's revenue is the total amount paid in by traders (via $C(\mathbf{q}_{\text{final}}) - C(\mathbf{q}_{\text{initial}})$), while its payout is determined by the outcome.

Let the true outcome be $k$. Then the AMM pays out $q_k$ shares at $1$ each. The profit is:

$$ \text{Profit} = C(\mathbf{q}_{\text{final}}) - C(\mathbf{0}) - q_k $$

Because prices are always slightly worse for the trader than the expected value under full information, the AMM makes money when traders are wrong or impatient.

Interpretation¶

  • Traders push $q_i$ up by buying shares, increasing $C(\mathbf{q})$.
  • When outcome $i$ doesn't happen, those shares are worthless.
  • Thus, bad forecasts fund the AMM’s profit.

This mechanism works without ever posting limit orders. The AMM simply offers take-it-or-leave-it prices derived from the curve above.

When Does the AMM Take the Trade?¶

In a Central Limit Order Book (CLOB), a trade occurs between:

  • A maker (who posts a standing order), and
  • A taker (who hits that order with a market/IOC order).

Polymarket has both:

  • User-posted limit orders (via the CLOB)
  • An AMM that provides standing liquidity, priced via LMSR.

The AMM only acts as taker, meaning it reacts to standing offers when they're better than its LMSR price.

Example 1: AMM Gets the Trade (Taker)¶

  • AMM LMSR price: \$0.60 for YES
  • A trader posts a limit sell of YES at \$0.55
  • \$0.55 is cheaper than \$0.60 → AMM buys from the trader

Result: Trader gives up value, AMM profits

Example 2: AMM Doesn't Get the Trade¶

  • AMM LMSR price: \$0.60 for YES
  • Trader posts a limit sell at \$0.62
  • AMM would not buy at that price → trade waits in the book

Result: Another trader might fill it, or it remains unfilled

General Rule:¶

The AMM only trades when: $$ \text{Standing price} \leq \text{LMSR price} \quad \text{(for buys)} $$ $$ \text{Standing price} \geq \text{LMSR price} \quad \text{(for sells)} $$

It always posts prices via the LMSR curve, but does not queue into the book. So:

  • You can beat the AMM by posting tighter prices
  • You can't front-run the AMM — it reacts instantly when price improves

Takeaway¶

  • The AMM earns edge when traders give it good prices
  • Otherwise, traders trade among themselves
  • You can analyze this by tagging trades:
    • maker == 'trader_1' or taker == 'trader_1' → AMM involvement

Layman’s Explanation of LMSR (Logarithmic Market Scoring Rule)¶

The LMSR is the rule used by the AMM (automated market maker) to set prices. It’s designed to always give you a price, no matter what, while protecting itself from unlimited loss.

Imagine This¶

You’re running a prediction market where people bet on whether YES or NO will happen.

Every time someone buys a share of YES, the AMM:

  • Increases the price of YES
  • Decreases the price of NO
  • Updates both prices based on how many shares of each have been bought

The Math Behind It¶

The AMM tracks how many shares have been bought:

  • $q_{\text{YES}}$: total YES shares bought
  • $q_{\text{NO}}$: total NO shares bought

The cost function is:

$$ C(q_{\text{YES}}, q_{\text{NO}}) = b \cdot \log\left( e^{q_{\text{YES}}/b} + e^{q_{\text{NO}}/b} \right) $$

Where:

  • $b$ is a constant that controls liquidity (higher $b$ = more liquidity, slower price movement)

The price of YES is the instantaneous cost to buy one more YES share:

$$ p_{\text{YES}} = \frac{e^{q_{\text{YES}}/b}}{e^{q_{\text{YES}}/b} + e^{q_{\text{NO}}/b}} $$

This means:

  • If people buy lots of YES → price of YES rises
  • If they buy NO → price of NO rises

Key Intuition¶

  1. You pay more the more you buy — price goes up with demand.
  2. You can always trade, but large trades are expensive.
  3. Only one side pays out, so the AMM makes money if the public is wrong.

Example¶

  • At the start: 0 shares bought → price = 50%
  • If someone buys 100 YES shares:
    • $q_{\text{YES}}$ increases
    • Price rises to maybe 65%
  • If YES ends up being correct, the AMM pays out \$1 per YES share
  • If NO wins, it pays nothing → AMM keeps the money paid in

Takeaway¶

LMSR is like a curve:

  • Smooth, no gaps
  • Always liquid
  • Becomes steeper as more shares are bought

It’s mathematically elegant and financially safe — traders can’t bankrupt the AMM.
